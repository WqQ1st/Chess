//**Board Representation**//
The board is represented by bitboards, one for each type of piece. Then the chessboard has a stack of boardstates that each store a
position, and those snapshots are stored within the one chessboard object.

//**Search Algorithm(s)**//
**Alpha Beta, fail-hard (current implementation)**:
Alpha is the lower bound; the worst the position eval can be. Beta is the opponent's guaranteed score from elsewhere in the tree, the worst
eval for the opponent. If playing a move returns beta, abandon that move bc the opponent has a counter/won't allow it. If a move gives
eval > alpha, then raise it. That means that the position is at least as good as the new alpha eval (assuming best move is played). 
Removes the need to search branches that are not feasible (eval >= beta, called "fail-hard"). If no better move is found, alpha is returned.

**Negamax Alpha Beta**:
Uses alpha beta pruning to always recursively maximize the score, but -negamax(board, -beta, -alpha, depth - 1) is called.
(every layer the side changes so maximize your score, minimize opponent's score) and your floor is their ceiling, your ceiling is their floor

**Quiescence Search**: 
Used to evaluate position more accurately than static; checks to see if doing nothing beats beta or raises alpha. Then recursively searches
captures and does static eval after that. solves horizon effect bc it goes through capture sequences and accounts for all hanging pieces.
Has no depth limit but exits once no captures exist

**Iterative Deepening**
Searches depth 1, 2, ..., depth - 1 before searching at depth in order to populate PV table, killer moves, history heuristic for the next
iteration. This is quicker than just immediately searching at depth (move sorting is important). Also allows for time management, when
stopping during an iteration due to time constraints, you can just return the results from the previous depth.

//**Move Sorting**//:
Putting moves that raise eval the most at the front of the list; then evaluating those moves first causes quicker beta cutoffs and less
moves to search. Hugely boosts speed

**Killer Moves**:
Used for move sorting; moves at a certain depth that gave high eval that can be searched early in other lines at the same depth.

**History Moves**:
Gives value to quiet (non-capture) moves of a certain piece type (ex: white pawn) to a certain square if moving it there raised alpha at
some point.

**Principal Variation (PV)**:
Stores the "best" line at each depth and prioritizes those, since they are likely to be strong moves even at a higher depth.

**Principal Variation Search (PVS) and Late Move Reduction (LMR)**
Principal variation search prioritizes the first move and performs a narrower search window [alpha, alpha + 1] on the rest to increase
efficiency (it asssumes the previously searched PV move will have the highest evaluation and re-searches if this is wrong). This works 
since the moves should be sorted with PV moves first, and works with LMR which reduces the depth of the search for moves that are
late in the move list. If the shallow depth finds a higher evaluation than before (> alpha), it is re-searched at full depth.

**Null Move Pruning**
Hits beta cutoff by allowing opponent to make a move and evaluating that position on reduced depth BEFORE generating moves. Quick and 
eliminates positions that are too good to be true. **assumes making no move and passing the turn to the opponent benefits opponent,
faulty when position is zugzwang**. Only runs when position is not in check.
